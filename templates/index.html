Introduction

Black pepper requires large quantities of nutrients to maintain favorable vegetative growth and yield. It has been estimated that the nutrient uptake of a mature stand of pepper amounts to 202Kg of N, 13Kg of P, 156kg of K, 18 kg of Mg and 68Kg of Ca per hectare. Pepper is mainly grown on the red yellow podzolic group of soils which have low to very low supply of plant nutrients and are very acidic. Adequate fertilizer application, soil conservation and other cultivation management are therefore essential for successful cultivation of this group.

1. Chlorosis- Nitrogen deficiency

Nitrogen deficiency is generally characterized by poor growth with pale and yellowish leaves. At first lower leaves turn yellowish but the upper canopy of affected vines tends to be greener. In severe cases leaves of the entire vine show a characteristic yellow to orange yellow discoloration and the extreme end of the leaf tip becomes necrotic in some instances.

2. Phosphorous deficiency

Clear symptoms are often rare in the field. In severe cases, the most striking symptom is stunted growth of the vine This effect is not so much of reduced terminal growth but more of restricted lateral growth due to poor secondary branching. Leaf blades of mature leaves become dull looking, turning bronzy in color.

3. Potassium deficiency

The distal end of affected mature leaf blades is necrotic, brittle and gray in color. Necrosis is usually confined to the distal end while the portion beyond the boundary, separating necrotic and live tissues, displays a ‘V’ shape band which is yellow to reddish brown color. This band sometimes occurs without the ‘tip burn’ symptom.

4. Magnesium deficiency

Symptoms first appear in older leaves and progress to younger leaves. In the early stage, chlorosis occurs in between main veins. This usually starts from the central proximal half of the leaf.
